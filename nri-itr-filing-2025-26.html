NRI ITR Filing 2025-26: Complete Step-by-Step Guide

By NRI Money Guide Team Last updated March 2026 7 min read
Key fact

NRIs are only taxed in India on income earned or accrued in India — not on their foreign salary or overseas savings. But if your Indian income exceeds ₹2.5 lakh in FY 2025-26, filing an ITR is mandatory. Miss the 31 July 2026 deadline and you face a penalty of up to ₹5,000 — plus interest on unpaid tax.

If you're an Indian professional living in Dubai, London, Toronto, or Singapore, the annual ITR filing season probably fills you with dread — or confusion. Does this even apply to me? What do I include? Will I be taxed on my salary abroad? These are real questions, and they deserve straight answers.

This guide covers everything you need to know about NRI ITR filing for Assessment Year 2025-26 (Financial Year 2025-26), including what changed after Budget 2025, which form to use, and how to actually file without hiring a CA for a simple return.

Who Must File an ITR as an NRI?

Not every NRI needs to file a tax return in India. The Income Tax Act keeps it fairly simple: you must file if any of the following apply to you for FY 2025-26:

  • Your India-sourced income exceeds ₹2.5 lakh in the financial year
  • You have TDS deducted on NRO account interest or a property sale and want a refund
  • You sold a property or shares in India and have capital gains to declare
  • You want to carry forward a capital loss to offset against future gains
  • You hold a signing authority in a foreign account (disclosure requirement)

Even if you owe zero tax — say, TDS was already deducted at source on your NRO interest — filing lets you claim a refund of any excess tax deducted. That alone is often reason enough.

Confirming Your Residential Status for AY 2025-26

Before filing anything, you need to confirm you are actually classified as an NRI under the Income Tax Act. The rules here are slightly different from FEMA (which governs your banking status) and can catch people off guard.

The basic test: days spent in India

You are a Resident for a given financial year if you spent 182 days or more in India during that year. Alternatively, you are a resident if you spent 60 or more days in India during the year AND 365 or more days across the preceding four years.

If you don't meet either condition, you are a Non-Resident Indian (NRI) for that year, and only your Indian-sourced income is taxable here. Foreign income stays outside India's tax net entirely.

One important exception: Indian citizens who work abroad (including seafarers) use the 182-day rule only — the 60-day rule does not apply to them. If you've always been abroad on a work visa, you almost certainly qualify as an NRI.

What Indian Income Do NRIs Pay Tax On?

As an NRI, only the following types of income attract Indian tax:

  • Rental income from property located in India
  • Interest on NRO accounts (NRE and FCNR interest is fully exempt)
  • Capital gains from selling Indian property, stocks, or mutual funds
  • Salary received in India for services rendered in India
  • Pension paid by an Indian employer
  • Dividend income from Indian companies

Your salary from your UAE, US, or UK employer is not taxable in India and does not need to appear in your Indian ITR. This is a common source of confusion — and a common mistake when people try to file without guidance.

What Budget 2025 Changed for NRI Filers

Budget 2025 made meaningful changes to the tax regime structure. The new tax regime (introduced in FY 2020-21) is now the default regime — you have to actively opt out if you want the old slab rates with deductions. Here is how the two regimes compare for NRI filers:

Income Slab New Regime (Default) Old Regime (Opt-in)
Up to ₹3,00,000 Nil Nil
₹3,00,001 – ₹7,00,000 5% Rebate u/s 87A 5% (up to ₹5L) / 20%
₹7,00,001 – ₹10,00,000 10% 20%
₹10,00,001 – ₹12,00,000 15% 30%
₹12,00,001 – ₹15,00,000 20% 30%
Above ₹15,00,000 30% 30%

The key advantage of the new regime for most NRIs is that income up to ₹7 lakh is effectively tax-free thanks to the rebate under Section 87A. However, NRIs cannot claim the Section 87A rebate if their income includes Special Rate income such as short-term capital gains taxed at 15% or 20%. Run the numbers on both regimes before selecting one.

Also notable: Budget 2025 rationalised TDS rates on NRO deposits, bringing them down from 30% to 20% for certain non-special income categories. This means less cash stuck in the TDS system before you can claim a refund.

Which ITR Form Should You Use?

Form Who Should Use It NRI Applicable?
ITR-1 (Sahaj) Salaried individuals, one house property, total income up to ₹50L No – NRIs excluded
ITR-2 NRIs with salary, rental income, capital gains, NRO interest, no business Yes – most NRIs
ITR-3 NRIs with business or professional income in India Yes – if business income
ITR-4 (Sugam) Presumptive income scheme Only if resident

The vast majority of NRIs will file using ITR-2. This covers rental income, NRO interest, capital gains from property or equity, and foreign income disclosure schedules.

How to File Your NRI ITR Online: Step by Step

The Income Tax Department's e-filing portal has improved significantly and most NRIs can file without professional help if their income is straightforward. Here is the process for AY 2025-26:

  1. Gather your documents: PAN card, Aadhaar (if linked), Form 26AS, AIS (Annual Information Statement), TDS certificates (Form 16A), bank statements for all NRO/NRE accounts, and sale/purchase deeds if you sold property.
  2. Log in to the e-filing portal: Go to incometax.gov.in and log in with your PAN and password. First-time users must register.
  3. Check Form 26AS and AIS first: Under 'e-File → Income Tax Return → View Form 26AS', verify all TDS entries. Cross-check with the AIS to catch any income the department already knows about. Discrepancies can trigger notices.
  4. Select the correct ITR form: Choose ITR-2 for most NRI situations (see table above). Select AY 2025-26.
  5. Choose your tax regime: Decide between new (default) and old regime. If you have significant deductions under 80C, 80D or home loan interest from Indian property, the old regime may still win. Otherwise, the new regime is usually simpler.
  6. Fill in your income details: Enter rental income under 'Income from House Property', NRO interest under 'Income from Other Sources', and capital gains in the relevant schedule. Leave foreign income out — you do not declare overseas salary here.
  7. Claim DTAA relief if applicable: If India has a tax treaty with your country of residence (UAE, US, UK, Canada, Singapore — all covered), you may be able to claim relief or exemption on certain incomes. Fill Schedule FSI and Schedule TR.
  8. Verify and submit: E-verify using Aadhaar OTP, net banking, or by sending a physical ITR-V to CPC Bengaluru within 30 days. E-verification is faster and recommended.

Key Deadlines for NRI ITR AY 2025-26

Deadline Date Applicable To
Standard filing deadline 31 July 2026 Most NRI filers
Audit cases deadline 31 October 2026 If tax audit required
Belated return deadline 31 December 2026 All filers (with penalty)
Updated return (ITR-U) Up to 2 years after end of AY Correction of errors

Filing a belated return (after 31 July) attracts a penalty of ₹5,000 under Section 234F. If your total income is below ₹5 lakh, the penalty is capped at ₹1,000. You also lose the ability to carry forward most capital losses if you miss the original deadline.

Common Mistakes NRIs Make When Filing

  • Using ITR-1: This form is not available to NRIs. Filing it will lead to a defective return notice.
  • Declaring foreign salary in the Indian ITR: As an NRI, your overseas income is not taxable in India and should not appear in your return.
  • Ignoring NRO account TDS: Banks deduct TDS at source on NRO interest. If your total income is below the taxable threshold, you're entitled to a full refund — but only if you file.
  • Not linking PAN with Aadhaar: Unlinked PANs attract higher TDS rates (20% instead of 10%) and can cause issues at the time of filing.
  • Forgetting to e-verify: Filing is not complete until verification. An unverified return is treated as not filed at all.
What this means for you
  • File ITR-2 if you have Indian income above ₹2.5 lakh, capital gains, or TDS to claim back — ITR-1 is not an option for NRIs.
  • Your foreign salary and overseas savings are not taxable in India and do not go in your return.
  • Budget 2025's new regime is now default — income up to ₹7 lakh is effectively tax-free, but run both regimes if you have Indian deductions.
  • Deadline is 31 July 2026. File on time to avoid the ₹5,000 penalty and to preserve the right to carry forward capital losses.
  • If India has a DTAA with your country (UAE, US, UK, Canada, Singapore), claim the treaty relief in Schedule FSI/TR to avoid double taxation.
  • E-verify your return immediately after submission — an unverified return doesn't count as filed.
